# Q

Often when I'm seeing the sun and the moon in the sky during the day,
the angle of the terminator on the moon doesn't seem to line up with where the sun is.
Am I right?

# A

Spot on! You’ve connected two of the most famous "sky illusions" out there. This is exactly the same psychological and geometric trap, often called the Moon Tilt Illusion.

Just like the contrail shadow, the issue isn't that physics is broken; it’s that your brain is trying to project a 3D reality onto a 2D "mental map" of the sky.

## The Great Circle Problem

We tend to think of the sky as a flat canvas, so we expect a straight line to connect the Sun and the Moon. In our minds, if the "lit" side of the Moon is pointing somewhere, we draw a straight 2D line from it and expect to hit the Sun.

However, the sky is a sphere. The shortest distance between two points on a sphere isn't a straight line—it’s a Great Circle (a curve).

Because you are standing inside that sphere, those curved lines look "wrong" to you. It’s like looking at a map of the world where airplane flight paths look like giant arcs; on a globe, those arcs are actually the most direct, straightest path.

## The Lack of Depth Cues

In the contrail example, you have two parallel lines at different depths. With the Moon and Sun, you have two objects at vastly different depths:

The Moon: ~238,000 miles away.

The Sun: ~93,000,000 miles away.

Your brain has no way to process that kind of distance, so it "plasters" both of them onto a single flat ceiling. When you try to connect them with a mental "string," your brain doesn't account for the curvature of the space between them.

## The "Frame of Reference" Error

When you look at the Moon, you likely orient your head and shoulders toward it. When you turn to look at the Sun, you rotate your body.

During that rotation, your internal "level"—your sense of what is "up" and "straight"—shifts. Because there are no straight lines in the sky (like the corner of a room or a horizon close to the objects), you lose the ability to maintain a consistent geometric grid. You think you’re looking at them "side-by-side," but you’re actually looking at two different points on a massive overhead dome.



How to "Fix" Your Brain

The next time you see the Moon and Sun together and the angle looks "wrong," try this trick to prove your intuition is just being fooled:

Find a straight edge: Use a long piece of string, a straight stick, or even a taut charging cable.

Hold it up: Hold the string at arm's length so it creates a perfectly straight line connecting the center of the Sun to the center of the Moon.

Check the alignment: You will see that the illuminated side of the Moon points exactly along that string to the Sun.

The Connection

In both cases—the contrail shadow and the Moon’s tilt—your brain is guilty of Linear Thinking in a Curvilinear World. It wants the universe to follow the rules of a flat piece of paper, but the sky demands the rules of a sphere.
